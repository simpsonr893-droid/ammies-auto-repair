5 U.S. Cities With the Highest Crime Rates — And What's Being Done

May 12, 2024 · 6 min read · By CrimeMap Research Team

When people search for crime statistics by city, they're usually trying to answer one simple question: is it safe to live here? Using our local crime tracker and block-level crime data from over 2,200 police departments, we analyzed 30-day and 12-month incident trends across every major U.S. metro.

1. Memphis, TN — Safety Score: 48/100

Memphis consistently ranks high on violent crime indexes. Our crime hotspot map reveals that the majority of incidents are concentrated in specific corridors — meaning most of the city is significantly safer than the headline numbers suggest. Memphis PD recently partnered with community organizations to expand real-time reporting, and our community safety alerts network in Memphis has grown 34% year-over-year.

2. St. Louis, MO — Safety Score: 51/100

St. Louis has one of the highest per-capita violent crime rates in the country, but the city's 12-month crime trend analysis shows a -3.7% decline — the largest single-year drop in a decade. The neighborhood safety app data shows the South City and Tower Grove areas outperforming citywide averages significantly.

3. Detroit, MI — Safety Score: 50/100

Detroit's transformation is real but uneven. CrimeMap's crime data visualization tools show sharp differences between neighborhoods — Midtown and Corktown score above 80, while some outer districts remain well below 50. Use the city crime map to explore block by block before visiting or relocating.

4. Baltimore, MD — Safety Score: 52/100

Baltimore's real-time crime alerts volume has decreased 2.7% over the last 12 months. Targeted intervention programs in the Cherry Hill and Sandtown neighborhoods have shown measurable impact in CrimeMap's data. The route safety planner remains one of our most-used features among Baltimore residents.

5. Albuquerque, NM — Safety Score: 55/100

Property crime drives Albuquerque's ranking more than violent crime. Our crime reporting app data shows vehicle theft hotspots concentrated near specific transit hubs. Real-time crime alerts in Albuquerque have helped users report incidents 40% faster than traditional 911 only approaches.

The key takeaway: crime statistics by city never tell the full story. Use CrimeMap's safety score and granular block-level crime data to understand exactly what's happening on your specific street — not just your city.

How to Use a Crime Map App to Find the Safest Neighborhood Before Moving

Apr 28, 2024 · 5 min read · By CrimeMap Editorial

Choosing where to live is one of the biggest decisions you'll make. A neighborhood safety app like CrimeMap gives you data that real estate listings never will. Here's exactly how to use it.

Step 1: Pull Up the Crime Hotspot Map

Open the crime hotspot map and enter the city you're considering. Switch on the heatmap overlay to see incident density at a glance. Zoom in to the specific neighborhoods on your shortlist and note which areas appear consistently cooler (safer) on the map.

Step 2: Check the Safety Score for Each Candidate Address

Every address in CrimeMap has a safety score from 0–100 calculated from 90 days of block-level crime data. Anything above 75 is considered low-risk. Between 60–74 is moderate. Below 60 warrants extra scrutiny. Look at the score AND the trend — a neighborhood scoring 65 but trending upward may be safer in a year than one scoring 70 but declining.

Step 3: Run the Route Safety Planner for Your Daily Commute

Your home address isn't the only location that matters. Use the route safety planner to map your daily commute routes — walking to transit, driving to work, jogging paths. A high safety rating at home loses value if you have to pass through a hotspot every morning.

Step 4: Set Up Real-Time Crime Alerts for a 30-Day Trial

Before committing, set your alert zone to the candidate address and live with the real-time crime alerts for 30 days. Volume and frequency of alerts tell you far more than any static statistic. You'll know exactly what you're moving into before you sign anything.

Step 5: Compare Crime Statistics by Neighborhood

Use the crime statistics by city comparison view to stack your top two or three neighborhoods side by side. Look at incident type breakdown — a neighborhood with more auto thefts than assaults has a very different character than the same score driven by violent crime.

Speed combined with context is what makes CrimeMap's alert system different from a simple notification. You don't just know something happened — you know what it means for your safety right now.

Understanding Your Neighborhood Safety Score: What the Numbers Mean

Apr 3, 2024 · 5 min read · By CrimeMap Data Team

CrimeMap assigns every block in every covered city a safety score from 0 to 100. It's one of the most-used features in our neighborhood safety app — and also one of the most misunderstood. Here's a plain-English breakdown of exactly what goes into it.

The Four Pillars of the Safety Score

1. Incident Volume (40%): The raw number of reported incidents on or within 300 feet of a block over the past 90 days, sourced from our police report tracker and community feeds.

2. Incident Severity (30%): Not all crimes are equal. Violent crimes like assault and robbery are weighted 3–5x higher than property crimes. This prevents a high-theft area from appearing equally dangerous as a high-assault area.

3. Trend Direction (20%): Our crime trend analysis compares current 30-day data against the prior 6-month average. A neighborhood improving rapidly receives a score boost; one deteriorating quickly receives a penalty.

4. Time-of-Day Pattern (10%): A block that's dangerous only between midnight and 4am scores differently than one where incidents are spread throughout the day. This helps the personal safety app give context-aware advice.

What the Ranges Mean

  • 85–100: Very Low Risk — ideal for families and solo walkers at any hour
  • 70–84: Low Risk — generally safe with normal awareness
  • 55–69: Moderate Risk — exercise standard urban caution
  • 40–54: Elevated Risk — avoid late-night walks; use the route planner
  • 0–39: High Risk — exercise significant caution; review alert settings

Your safety rating updates every hour. Check it before any unfamiliar trip — and use the trend indicator to see which direction your neighborhood is heading.

Route Safety Planning: Walk, Run & Drive Smarter in Any City

Mar 22, 2024 · 5 min read · By CrimeMap Product Team

The route safety planner is CrimeMap's most talked-about premium feature — and for good reason. It's the first navigation tool that scores routes by neighborhood safety rather than just travel time.

How It Works

Enter your origin and destination. CrimeMap generates up to five route alternatives and overlays each with safety scores drawn from real-time block-level crime data. Routes are color-coded: green (score 75+), yellow (55–74), and red (below 55). You choose your safe route and navigate using your preferred maps app.

For Walkers and Runners

Pedestrians face unique risks that drivers don't. The route planner accounts for this by weighting pedestrian-specific incident types — muggings, harassment, assault — more heavily for walking routes. It also factors in lighting data and whether streets are commercial (more foot traffic, safer) or isolated residential.

For Cyclists

CrimeMap's cycling route mode combines safety scoring with bike-specific infrastructure data. A route might score high on safety but low on bike-friendliness — the planner shows both dimensions so you can make the right tradeoff.

For Drivers

Driving at night through unfamiliar areas? The route planner flags crime hotspot areas along your route and suggests detours. It's particularly useful for rideshare drivers and delivery workers navigating high-incident zones on tight schedules.

The route safety planner updates every time new community safety alerts come in — so if an incident is reported on your planned route mid-journey, you'll get a reroute suggestion in real time.

Crime Trend Analysis: Is Your City Getting Safer? Here's How to Find Out

Feb 28, 2024 · 5 min read · By CrimeMap Data Team

Headlines about crime are almost always backward-looking — they tell you what happened last year, not what's happening on your street right now. CrimeMap's crime trend analysis tools flip this completely.

30-Day vs. 12-Month Trends

CrimeMap tracks two trend windows simultaneously. The 30-day trend shows recent momentum — is crime spiking this month? The 12-month trend shows structural change — is the neighborhood genuinely improving? A city can have a bad month but a strong annual trajectory. Our crime data visualization lets you see both at once with clear charts you can actually interpret.

Incident Type Breakdown

Overall crime numbers hide important nuances. A neighborhood where auto theft is rising but violent crime is falling has a very different character than one where the opposite is true. The local crime tracker breaks down every trend by incident type, so you understand the nature of the change — not just the direction.

Comparing Neighborhoods Side by Side

Use the comparison view to stack up to four neighborhoods. See safety scores, 30-day incident counts, and trend arrows for each. This is the most used feature for people doing pre-move research and for local journalists covering crime statistics by city.

Setting Trend Alerts

You can set a trend alert for any neighborhood: if the 30-day incident count rises more than 15% above the 6-month average, CrimeMap will notify you immediately. This is invaluable for property owners, business operators, and anyone who wants to stay ahead of neighborhood changes rather than react to them.

Data doesn't lie — but it requires context. CrimeMap's crime trend analysis gives you both the numbers and the story behind them, so you can make genuinely informed decisions about where you live, work, and travel.
