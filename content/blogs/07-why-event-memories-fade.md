# Why Event Memories Fade — And the One Thing That Helps

*Brand: Instant Camera for Events | Category: Reflection*

---

Memory is a bad archivist.

The science on this is not encouraging. Within 24 hours of an event, people forget about 70% of what they experienced. Within a week, that number climbs toward 90%. The details go first — the color of the tablecloths, the song that was playing when two people danced for the first time, the exact words of the toast that made the whole room quiet.

What stays longer are the emotional impressions. You remember that the night felt warm and easy. You remember that something important happened at the table by the window. You remember that someone said something that mattered. But the specifics — those blur fast.

Photos are the exception.

Not because they preserve everything, but because they preserve something. A good photo doesn't just show you what happened — it triggers the parts of the memory that are still there. The photo of someone's face brings back the sound of their voice. The photo of the dessert table brings back the smell of the venue. One specific image can unlock a cascade of things you thought you'd forgotten.

This is why photos taken at events are worth keeping, worth organizing, worth sharing quickly — while the memories they're anchored to are still intact enough to give them meaning.

## The problem with waiting

Most event photos don't get shared for days, sometimes weeks. People mean to create an album, to send the gallery link, to post the highlights. And the longer they wait, the less it matters — because the memories the photos were meant to reinforce have already started to blur.

By the time the album goes out, the photos feel like documents rather than souvenirs.

**Instant Camera for Events** is built around the opposite logic: share while you're still there. Share before you drive home. Share while you can still remember exactly what was happening when the photo was taken.

The sharing is instant. The Web Share API sends photos through your phone's native share sheet — to a group chat, to a story, to a direct message — without leaving the app, without uploading to a server, without waiting for anything to process.

## Photos as memory infrastructure

The best thing about a photo from a good event isn't what it shows. It's what it unlocks. Years from now, someone will find a photo from tonight in their camera roll and remember everything — the conversation they had, the person they met, the moment they didn't expect to mean as much as it did.

That's worth thirty seconds with a camera.
